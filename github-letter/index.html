Hello, GitHubber!

I'm Brent and I want to work with you.

I live in Florida with my wife and cat. I'm a Rubyist, frontender, Cocoa developer, and sometimes a designer. I've been making software professionally for about 6 years.

Here's a bit about me, and why I want to work at GitHub.

First, an abbreviated work history

2008
At Spatial Networks, a geospatial company here in Tampa Bay, I worked within a very small team to create the REST API, Rails app, and iOS client of the first versions of what is now called Fulcrum, a flexible POI collection tool. This is where my love of Ruby blossomed, where I wrote my first line of Objective-C, where I discovered that map projections are mind-blowing, and where we chose MongoDB to store the app's highly heterogeneous data.
2011
I went on to work remotely at Zaarly, an SF-based startup creating a marketplace where people can make money doing what they love. Here I worked on their JavaScript-heavy frontend, put together their CSS styleguide with my fork of KSS (thanks @kneath!), worked on the iOS app, and built their peer-to-peer payments system using Balanced. Oh, and I became addicted to Blue Bottle, being that there's one right next door to their office.
Now
Around 8 months ago I began freelancing, and ended up going from contract to full-time with the great folks at Kajabi, a bootstrapped & profitable company specializing in products for Internet marketers. Most recently we shipped the freshly minted Megaphone, an app that enables painless creation of inbound marketing pages.

In short, I am a well-rounded software engineer with experience across the spectrum of modern web and mobile development, frontend to backend. I prefer it this way—I find it immensely satisfying to be able to take an idea from the back of a napkin, through data modeling, UI design, and code, and end up with a beautiful, functional piece of software.

Lately, things have been great. While creating software is still my passion, I admit that I no longer spend 12+ hours a day in front of my laptop. Within the past year I’ve taken up guitar, a foreign language, cooking, cycling, and yoga. I'm not particularly good at any of these things yet, but I have fun thinking of myself as a sort of crappy renaissance man.

It seems I've finally achieved a great work/life balance. But despite this, it feels like something is missing.

I can't stop thinking about { Developer Tools }

Amazing latte art by Yuko Honda.

Not long ago I created Xray, a Rails development tool that scratches an itch I've had for a long time, and I can't stop thinking about more. I dream of futuristic light tables for developers (yes, seriously), a text editor that integrates with Git and GitHub in ways that tacked-on plugins can't, or just simple ways to improve the interactive diff staging in GitHub for Mac that, for me, immediately replaced git add -i.

I want to work on what I dream about.

The most empowering thing I find about being a developer is that we're able to craft the tools we use every day. As a community, we're in complete control of whether or not we're as effective—and happy—as we can be when making software.

And that's why GitHub makes me smile: GitHub creates, and is the home of, most of the tools I use daily to be a better, happier developer, and the internal tools you've built show that you understand how important tooling really is. To me, GitHub stands for the idea that with the right tools, through collaboration and constant improvement, together we can create a better world of software, and maybe even more.

Truth be told, the only other company that has captured my heart the way GitHub has is Apple. GitHub didn't invent open source or version control, but like Apple's products, it's hard to imagine where we'd be without GitHub's vision and execution of those ideas.

I want to to refocus my career on building the tools of the future that make other software creators smile, and I can't imagine a better place to do that than within GitHub's famous culture of doers.
